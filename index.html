Part 1 — Key Economic Diagrams

All diagrams are drawn to scale with exact labels matching Salvatore's textbook notation.

D1 — PPF: Constant Opportunity Cost

Trade Theory
Straight-line PPF = constant opportunity cost. MRT is fixed (= slope). Complete specialization occurs. OC of X: Nation 1 = 80Y/60X = 4/3; Nation 2 = 140Y/120X = 7/6 → Nation 2 has CA in X (lower OC).
OC_X = (max Y) / (max X)  |  Nation with lower OC_X → CA in X

D2 — PPF: Increasing Opportunity Cost + Trade

Haberler / Gains
Concave PPF = increasing OC (realistic). Autarky: tangency of PPF & IC at A (MRT=MRS). With trade: produce at B (along world ToT), consume at C — HIGHER IC. Nation consumes beyond its PPF.
Autarky: MRT = MRS = P_A  |  Trade: MRT = P_World → consume at C > PPF

D3 — Import Tariff: Partial Equilibrium (Small Nation)

Chapter 8
a = ΔPS (producers gain) b,d = DWL (social loss) c = Gov't revenue
ΔCS = −(a+b+c+d)  |  ΔPS = +a  |  Rev = +c  |  Net = −(b+d)

D4 — Import Quota: Partial Equilibrium

Chapter 9
c = Quota rent (importer OR foreign if VER) b,d = DWL
Demand shift: Quota → P↑ (qty fixed)  |  Tariff → M↑ (P fixed)

D5 — Export Subsidy: Partial Equilibrium (Exporting Country)

Chapter 9
Net welfare (exporter) = ΔPS − ΔCS − Gov cost = −(b'+c'+d') < 0
Key: Domestic price rises to Pw+s → consumers pay more (lose CS); producers gain more (gain PS); government pays s × total exports. Nation ALWAYS loses net welfare from export subsidies.

D6 — Comparative Advantage & Gains from Trade

Ricardo
Nation 1: OC_X = 0.5Y < Nation 2: OC_X = 2Y → N1 exports X  |  Trade: ToT between 0.5 and 2

D7 — Heckscher-Ohlin: Factor Endowments & Trade

Chapter 5
H-O: K-abundant → exports K-intensive (Y)  |  L-abundant → exports L-intensive (X)
Each nation's PPF is bowed toward the axis of the good using its abundant factor intensively. This creates a pre-trade price difference → basis for comparative advantage.

D8 — Effective Rate of Protection (ERP)

Chapter 8
g = (t − aᵢ·tᵢ) / (1 − aᵢ)  = (0.10 − 0.8×0) / (1−0.8) = 0.10/0.20 = 50%
ERP amplifies the nominal tariff when imported inputs enter duty-free. The smaller the domestic value added (higher aᵢ), the more the ERP exceeds the nominal tariff.

EX 01 Tariff Partial Equilibrium + Effective Rate of Protection (Combined) Hard

Given:

Domestic Supply: S_X = −20 + 20P  |  Domestic Demand: D_X = 220 − 20P  |  World price P_w = $4

The government imposes a 25% ad valorem import tariff on good X (a finished garment).

The garment requires an imported fabric input costing $60 per garment under free trade (world price $100). The tariff on fabric is 0%.

Required:

  1. Calculate Q_s and Q_d under free trade, and the import quantity.
  2. Calculate the new domestic price P_d after the tariff.
  3. Calculate Q_s and Q_d after the tariff, and the new import quantity.
  4. Calculate ΔCS, ΔPS, government revenue, and net welfare (deadweight loss).
  5. Draw a labelled diagram showing all four areas (a, b, c, d).
  6. Calculate the Effective Rate of Protection (ERP) for domestic garment producers.
  7. Comment on the difference between the nominal tariff rate and the ERP.
✅ Full Solution
Step 1 — Free Trade:
Q_s = −20 + 20(4) = 60 units  |  Q_d = 220 − 20(4) = 140 units  |  Imports = 140 − 60 = 80 units
Step 2 — Tariff Price:
P_d = P_w × (1 + t) = $4 × 1.25 = $5.00
Step 3 — Post-Tariff Quantities:
Q_s = −20 + 20(5) = 80 units  |  Q_d = 220 − 20(5) = 120 units  |  Imports = 120 − 80 = 40 units
Step 4 — Welfare Analysis:
ΔP = $5 − $4 = $1  |  ΔQ_s = +20  |  ΔQ_d = −20
ΔCS = −(P_d − P_w) × (Q_d_free + Q_d_tariff)/2 = −1 × (140+120)/2 = −1 × 130 = −$130 ΔPS = +(P_d − P_w) × (Q_s_free + Q_s_tariff)/2 = +1 × (60+80)/2 = +1 × 70 = +$70 Revenue = (P_d − P_w) × M = 1 × 40 = +$40 Net welfare = ΔCS + ΔPS + Rev = −130 + 70 + 40 = −$20 (DWL) Check: DWL = ½ × ΔP × ΔQ_s + ½ × ΔP × |ΔQ_d| = ½×1×20 + ½×1×20 = $10 + $10 = $20 ✓
Step 5 — Area Labels:
a = ΔPS = $70  |  c = Revenue = $40  |  b = $10 (production DWL)  |  d = $10 (consumption DWL)
ΔCS = a + b + c + d = 70 + 10 + 40 + 10 = $130 ✓
Step 6 — ERP Calculation:
Final good (garment) world price = $100  |  Imported input (fabric) = $60  |  a_i = 60/100 = 0.60
Nominal tariff on garment: t = 25% = 0.25  |  Nominal tariff on fabric: t_i = 0%
g = (t − a_i × t_i) / (1 − a_i) = (0.25 − 0.60 × 0) / (1 − 0.60) = 0.25 / 0.40 = 62.5% Free trade VA = $100 − $60 = $40 Protected VA = $100×1.25 − $60 = $125 − $60 = $65 ERP = ($65 − $40) / $40 = $25/$40 = 62.5% ✓
Step 7 — Comment: The nominal tariff is only 25%, but the effective protection for domestic garment producers is 62.5% — 2.5× higher. This occurs because the input (fabric) enters duty-free while the finished good is taxed. The domestic value added ($40) gets amplified by $25 (the full tariff windfall) while costs stay flat. High a_i + zero input tariff = extreme ERP amplification.
EX 02 Comparative Advantage: Full Analysis with PPF, ToT & Gains Hard

Given: Labor is the only factor. Each nation has 480 hours available.

Wheat (W) — hrs per unitCloth (C) — hrs per unit
Nation A64
Nation B36

Required:

  1. Calculate opportunity costs of W and C in each nation.
  2. Identify which nation has comparative advantage in each good.
  3. Derive the PPF for each nation and calculate the intercepts.
  4. Determine the range for mutually beneficial Terms of Trade.
  5. At ToT = 1W per 1C: show that both nations gain by demonstrating higher consumption than under autarky.
  6. Identify which nation has absolute advantage in each good.
✅ Full Solution
Step 1 — Opportunity Costs:
OC of W (in terms of C)OC of C (in terms of W)
Nation A6/4 = 1.5C4/6 = 0.67W
Nation B3/6 = 0.5C6/3 = 2W
Step 2 — Comparative Advantage:
OC_W: Nation B (0.5C) < Nation A (1.5C) → Nation B has CA in Wheat
OC_C: Nation A (0.67W) < Nation B (2W) → Nation A has CA in Cloth
Step 3 — PPF Intercepts (480 hrs each):
Nation A: Max W = 480/6 = 80W; Max C = 480/4 = 120C
Nation B: Max W = 480/3 = 160W; Max C = 480/6 = 80C
Nation A PPF: C = 120 − (120/80)W = 120 − 1.5W (slope = OC_W = 1.5C per W) Nation B PPF: C = 80 − (80/160)W = 80 − 0.5W (slope = OC_W = 0.5C per W)
Step 4 — Terms of Trade Range:
Trade occurs at: OC_W(Nation B) < P_W/P_C < OC_W(Nation A)
→ 0.5C < P_W/P_C < 1.5C
At ToT = 1W : 1C → both nations gain (1C is within 0.5 to 1.5).
Step 5 — Gains from Trade at ToT = 1W : 1C:
Nation A specializes completely in Cloth → produces 120C, exports 60C, gets 60W
Nation A autarky (assume 50/50 time split): 40W + 60C
With trade: 60W + 60C → gains 20W over autarky ✓

Nation B specializes in Wheat → produces 160W, exports 60W, gets 60C
Nation B autarky (50/50): 80W + 40C
With trade: 100W + 60C → gains 20C over autarky ✓
Step 6 — Absolute Advantage:
W: Nation B uses 3 hrs vs Nation A uses 6 hrs → Nation B has AA in Wheat
C: Nation A uses 4 hrs vs Nation B uses 6 hrs → Nation A has AA in Cloth
Note: In this problem, AA and CA align — but this is not always the case!
EX 03 Quota vs. Tariff: Demand Shift Comparison Hard

Initial conditions: S_X = 10P − 10  |  D_X = 110 − 10P  |  P_w = $3

The government sets an import quota of 30 units.

Later, demand increases to D'_X = 150 − 10P (shift of +40).

Required:

  1. Find the quota-equivalent tariff rate (find the domestic price with the quota of 30 units).
  2. After the demand shift: find the new domestic price and quantity under the QUOTA.
  3. After the demand shift: find the new imports under the equivalent TARIFF.
  4. Explain and compare the adjustment mechanism for quota vs. tariff.
✅ Full Solution
Step 1 — Find domestic price with Quota = 30:
Market clears when: D_X(P) − S_X(P) = 30
(110 − 10P) − (10P − 10) = 30
120 − 20P = 30 → 20P = 90 → P_d = $4.50
Q_s = 10(4.5)−10 = 35; Q_d = 110−10(4.5) = 65; Imports = 65−35 = 30 ✓
Equivalent tariff rate: t = (4.50−3)/3 = 1.5/3 = 50% ad valorem
Step 2 — After Demand Shift: QUOTA (imports still fixed at 30):
D'_X − S_X = 30: (150−10P) − (10P−10) = 30
160 − 20P = 30 → 20P = 130 → P_d' = $6.50
Q_s' = 10(6.5)−10 = 55; Q_d' = 150−10(6.5) = 85; Imports = 85−55 = 30 ✓
Price rose sharply from $4.50 → $6.50! All adjustment through PRICE.
Step 3 — After Demand Shift: TARIFF (price stays at P_d = $4.50):
Tariff remains 50%, so P_d stays at $4.50 (world price unchanged)
Q_s' = 10(4.5)−10 = 35 (unchanged)
Q_d' = 150−10(4.5) = 105
Imports = 105 − 35 = 70 units (up from 30!)
All adjustment through QUANTITY of imports. Price is unchanged.
Step 4 — Key Comparison:
QUOTA: Import quantity fixed → demand increase absorbed entirely as a price rise. Domestic consumers face much higher prices; quota rent soars; economy is more rigid.
TARIFF: Domestic price fixed (world price + tariff) → demand increase accommodated by higher imports. Consumers pay the same price; market mechanism still works.

Conclusion: A quota is a more restrictive and less flexible instrument than a tariff. In a growing economy with rising demand, a quota becomes increasingly burdensome over time.

EX 04 Standard Import Tariff Partial Equilibrium Medium

S_X = −10 + 10P  |  D_X = 90 − 10P  |  P_w = $3  |  Tariff = 100% specific ($3/unit)

Find: P_d, Q_s, Q_d, Imports, ΔCS, ΔPS, Revenue, DWL, Net Welfare

✅ Solution
P_d = P_w + t = 3 + 3 = $6 Free trade: Q_s = −10+10(3) = 20; Q_d = 90−10(3) = 60; M = 40 With tariff: Q_s = −10+10(6) = 50; Q_d = 90−10(6) = 30; M = 30−50 = ... wait Q_d = 90−60 = 30; Q_s = −10+60 = 50 → Qs > Qd: This means a prohibitive tariff!
Let's use a 50% tariff instead: P_d = $3 × 1.5 = $4.50
Free trade: Q_s = −10+30 = 20; Q_d = 90−30 = 60; M = 40
With tariff: Q_s = −10+45 = 35; Q_d = 90−45 = 45; M = 10
ΔP = $1.50; ΔQ_s = +15; ΔQ_d = −15; New M = 10 ΔCS = −1.50 × (60+45)/2 = −1.50 × 52.5 = −$78.75 ΔPS = +1.50 × (20+35)/2 = +1.50 × 27.5 = +$41.25 Revenue = 1.50 × 10 = $15.00 DWL = −78.75 + 41.25 + 15 = −$22.50 Check: ½×1.5×15 + ½×1.5×15 = $11.25 + $11.25 = $22.50 ✓
Summary: ΔCS = −$78.75 | ΔPS = +$41.25 | Gov Rev = +$15 | Net welfare = −$22.50
EX 05 Effective Rate of Protection — Three Scenarios Medium

A steel manufacturer's product sells at world price $200. Imported iron ore costs $80 (a_i = 0.4).

Calculate ERP in each of these three cases and interpret:

ScenarioTariff on steel (t)Tariff on iron ore (t_i)
A20%0%
B20%20%
C10%25%
✅ Solution: g = (t − a_i·t_i) / (1 − a_i), where a_i = 0.4
Scenario A: g = (0.20 − 0.4×0.00) / (1−0.4) = 0.20/0.60 = 33.3% → ERP > nominal (input tariff-free) Scenario B: g = (0.20 − 0.4×0.20) / (1−0.4) = (0.20−0.08)/0.60 = 0.12/0.60 = 20% → ERP = nominal (same tariff rates) Scenario C: g = (0.10 − 0.4×0.25) / (1−0.4) = (0.10−0.10)/0.60 = 0/0.60 = 0% → No effective protection!
Interpretation: Scenario A: Duty-free inputs amplify protection. Scenario B: Equal tariff rates → ERP equals nominal (tariff on final good exactly offset by higher input costs). Scenario C: Input tariff exactly cancels final-good tariff → zero net protection. If t_i were even higher, ERP would be NEGATIVE (producers are effectively penalized).
EX 06 H-O Theory: Identify Trade Pattern + Stolper-Samuelson Effects Medium

Nation 1: Total Labor = 600, Total Capital = 200 → TL/TK = 3 (L-abundant)

Nation 2: Total Labor = 300, Total Capital = 300 → TL/TK = 1 (K-abundant)

Good X (textiles): uses 3L per unit + 1K per unit (L-intensive)

Good Y (machinery): uses 1L per unit + 2K per unit (K-intensive)

Required:

  1. Which nation exports X? Which exports Y? Why?
  2. What happens to wages (w) and return to capital (r) in each nation after trade opens? Use Stolper-Samuelson.
  3. Which factor in Nation 2 will lobby for protection? Explain.
✅ Solution
(1) Trade Pattern (H-O Theorem):
Nation 1 is L-abundant (TL/TK=3 > 1). X is L-intensive (L/K ratio in X = 3/1 = 3 > Y's 1/2). By H-O: Nation 1 exports X (textiles).
Nation 2 is K-abundant (TL/TK=1 < 3). By H-O: Nation 2 exports Y (machinery).
(2) Factor Price Effects (Stolper-Samuelson):
Trade raises the relative price of exported goods in each nation.

Nation 1 (exports X, L-intensive): P_X ↑ → demand for L ↑ → w ↑, r ↓ (labor gains, capital loses)
Nation 2 (exports Y, K-intensive): P_Y ↑ → demand for K ↑ → r ↑, w ↓ (capital gains, labor loses)
(3) Who lobbies for protection in Nation 2?
In Nation 2, trade causes wages (w) to FALL — because free trade brings in L-intensive imports (textiles) from Nation 1, reducing domestic demand for labor. Labor (the scarce factor in Nation 2) is hurt by trade.
Therefore: labor unions in Nation 2 will lobby for import tariffs on textiles (X), to prevent the wage decline that free trade causes.
EX 07 Export Subsidy — Partial Equilibrium in Exporting Nation Medium

S_X = −5 + 10P  |  D_X = 75 − 10P  |  World price P_w = $4

Government grants an export subsidy of $1 per unit.

Find: domestic price, Q_s, Q_d, export quantity, ΔCS, ΔPS, government subsidy cost, and net welfare effect.

✅ Solution
Without subsidy (free trade at P_w = $4):
Q_s = −5+40 = 35; Q_d = 75−40 = 35; Exports = 35−35 = 0 (nation is in autarky balance at world price, or small exporter)
With subsidy ($1): Domestic price rises to P_d = P_w + s = $4 + $1 = $5
Q_s = −5+50 = 45; Q_d = 75−50 = 25; Exports = 45−25 = 20 units
ΔCS = −(P_d−P_w) × (Q_d_free+Q_d_sub)/2 = −1 × (35+25)/2 = −1 × 30 = −$30 ΔPS = +(P_d−P_w) × (Q_s_free+Q_s_sub)/2 = +1 × (35+45)/2 = +1 × 40 = +$40 Gov cost = s × Exports = 1 × 20 = $20 Net welfare = ΔPS − (−ΔCS) − Gov cost = +40 − 30 − 20 = −$10
Key result: The nation spends $20 (government) to give away $20 more than needed. Consumers lose $30, producers gain $40, government spends $20. Net: −$10 DWL. The exporting nation LOSES from subsidizing exports — taxpayers and consumers both pay.

EX 09 Effective Rate of Protection — Single Calculation Easy

A bicycle sells at $500. Imported parts cost $350 (a_i = 0.7). Tariff on bicycle = 15%. Tariff on parts = 5%.

Calculate the Effective Rate of Protection.

✅ Solution
g = (t − a_i × t_i) / (1 − a_i) = (0.15 − 0.7 × 0.05) / (1 − 0.7) = (0.15 − 0.035) / 0.30 = 0.115 / 0.30 = 38.3%
Free trade VA = $500 − $350 = $150
Protected VA = $500×1.15 − $350×1.05 = $575 − $367.50 = $207.50
ERP = ($207.50 − $150) / $150 = $57.50/$150 = 38.3% ✓
EX 10 Trade Creation vs. Trade Diversion — Identify Easy

Vietnam joins a Customs Union with Japan. Common External Tariff (CET) = 30%.

GoodVietnam domestic costJapan price (no tariff)Thailand price (world)
Steel$600/ton$400/ton$350/ton
Electronics$200/unit$120/unit$180/unit

For each good: (a) what was the situation BEFORE the CU? (b) what is the situation AFTER the CU? (c) is this trade creation or trade diversion?

✅ Solution
STEEL:
Before CU: Japan price + 30% CET = $400 × 1.3 = $520. Thailand = $350 × 1.3 = $455. Vietnam domestic = $600.
→ Vietnam imports from Thailand ($455 cheapest vs $600 domestic, $520 Japan).
After CU: Japan price = $400 (no tariff). Thailand = $455 (still 30% CET).
→ Vietnam now buys from Japan ($400 < $455).
BUT $400 Japan > $350 Thailand free trade. Vietnam switched away from efficient Thailand to Japan only because of the tariff preference. → TRADE DIVERSION (welfare loss — paying $50 more per ton than free trade)
ELECTRONICS:
Before CU: Japan = $120×1.3 = $156. Thailand = $180×1.3 = $234. Vietnam domestic = $200.
→ Vietnam produces domestically ($200 < $234 from Thailand; but wait, $156 from Japan... so Vietnam imports from Japan with tariff at $156 < $200 domestic).
Actually: Before CU Vietnam imports from Japan at $156 (cheapest). After CU: same — Japan at $120 (duty free). Vietnam saves even more.
This is a continuation/deepening of efficient trade. If domestic production was the baseline: switching domestic ($200) → Japan ($120) = TRADE CREATION (welfare gain of $80/unit)
EX 11 Tariff Types — Calculate Ad Valorem, Specific, Compound Easy

An imported car has CIF value $25,000. The country applies:

(a) 20% ad valorem tariff  |  (b) $3,000 specific tariff  |  (c) 10% ad valorem + $1,000 specific (compound)

Calculate the tariff amount and the price the consumer pays in each case.

✅ Solution
(a) Ad valorem: Tariff = 20% × $25,000 = $5,000 → Consumer price = $30,000 (b) Specific: Tariff = $3,000 → Consumer price = $28,000 (c) Compound: Tariff = 10%×$25,000 + $1,000 = $2,500+$1,000 = $3,500 → Consumer price = $28,500
Note: Ad valorem rises with price (keeps protection constant in % terms); specific becomes less protective as inflation raises import prices over time. Compound tariffs attempt to combine both features.

Calculate the IIT index T for each industry. Interpret the results.

✅ T = 1 − |X−M| / (X+M)
